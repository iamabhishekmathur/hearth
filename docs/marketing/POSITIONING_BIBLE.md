# Hearth Positioning Bible

Status: Working source of truth  
Last updated: 2026-04-27  
Purpose: Align Hearth's website, README, docs, launch content, product video, social presence, outreach, and sales conversations around one clear strategic story.

## 1. Strategic Center

### Core insight

Teams are already doing useful work with AI, but the value is fragmented across people, tools, chat histories, docs, meetings, task boards, and one-off automations.

When one teammate finds a better way to work with AI, that breakthrough usually does not become the rest of the team's starting point. Hearth exists to make that happen.

### Strategy one-liner

Hearth turns individual AI leverage into shared organizational capability.

### Customer-facing one-liner

Hearth helps teams turn fragmented AI usage into reusable, governed workflows.

### Recommended hero

H1:

> Make your AI power users' breakthroughs your team's starting point.

Subhead:

> Hearth is an open-source AI workspace that turns useful workflows, context, and decisions into shared systems your team can reuse, improve, and govern.

Supporting section line:

> When one teammate finds a better way to work with AI, everyone should benefit.

### Short pitch

Hearth is an open-source AI workspace for teams. It helps AI power users turn useful prompts, workflows, context, tasks, and decisions into shared systems the whole team can reuse, while giving technical teams control over governance, memory, and infrastructure.

### Longer pitch

Most teams already have people getting real leverage from AI, but the work is scattered across ChatGPT, Claude, Slack, meetings, docs, task tools, and one-off automations. Hearth gives teams one place to collaborate with AI, capture what worked, turn follow-up into tasks and routines, preserve decisions and context, and keep organizational AI memory in infrastructure they control.

## 2. What Hearth Is

Hearth is:

- An open-source AI workspace for teams.
- A way to make useful AI work reusable across the organization.
- A shared place for AI-assisted work, decisions, tasks, routines, memory, and activity.
- A governed path between shadow AI and AI lockdown.
- A self-hostable platform for teams that want control over their data, providers, memory, and workflows.

Hearth is not:

- A generic chatbot.
- A prettier wrapper around ChatGPT.
- A project management tool with AI sprinkled on top.
- A compliance-only product.
- A research tool for running AI experiments.
- Employee surveillance software.
- A "digital twin" product as the primary story.

## 3. Best-Fit Customer

### Launch ICP

AI-forward teams where a few people are already creating real leverage with AI, but the organization has no repeatable way to spread that leverage.

Likely early segments:

- Technical founders and startup teams.
- CTOs and engineering/product/ops leaders at small to mid-sized companies.
- Internal AI champions and power users.
- Chief of staff, operations, product, engineering, support, recruiting, finance, and marketing teams with repeatable knowledge work.
- Teams that care about self-hosting, open source, provider choice, or governance.

### Strong buying signals

- Multiple people already use ChatGPT, Claude, Cursor, Perplexity, or other AI tools for daily work.
- Prompts, workflows, or "AI tips" are being shared manually in Slack, docs, or meetings.
- The team has recurring work that could become reusable routines.
- Leaders worry that useful AI work disappears when it stays in individual tools.
- IT/security worries about shadow AI, vendor lock-in, sensitive context, or auditability.
- Teams want AI adoption but do not want to give one vendor the entire organizational memory layer.

### Weak initial fit

- Teams that are not using AI at all yet.
- Teams looking only for a single-purpose chatbot.
- Teams that want a fully managed enterprise suite with no self-hosting or technical setup.
- Organizations that will not tolerate open-source/self-hosted software in any form.

## 4. Audience Map

### First user: AI power user / champion

This person is already getting leverage from AI. They have discovered better prompts, workflows, automations, research patterns, analysis loops, or decision processes.

What they care about:

- "I found a better way to work. How do I share it without becoming tech support?"
- "How do I turn this into something reusable?"
- "How do I help teammates start from what already works?"

Message:

> Make your AI breakthroughs your team's starting point.

### Team lead / operator

This person cares about output, follow-through, repeatability, and knowledge transfer.

What they care about:

- Scattered context across meetings, docs, Slack, tasks, and AI chats.
- Decisions disappearing after the meeting.
- Follow-up not making it into the task board.
- Every teammate starting from a blank AI conversation.

Message:

> Capture the AI workflows, decisions, and follow-up your team already creates, then turn them into repeatable workflows.

### Mass user: knowledge worker

This person does not want to become a prompt engineer. They want a useful starting point.

What they care about:

- "Show me the workflow that works."
- "Help me use what my teammate figured out."
- "Give me the context behind this decision."
- "Turn this conversation into next steps."

Message:

> Start from your team's best AI workflows instead of a blank chat.

### Admin / CTO / IT / InfoSec

This person cares about control, governance, security, auditability, cost, and vendor lock-in.

What they care about:

- Shadow AI spreading across the company.
- Blanket AI lockdown slowing the business down.
- Company context accumulating inside external AI vendors.
- Data controls, provider choice, audit logs, access controls, and deployment model.

Message:

> Govern AI adoption without blocking the teams already finding value.

### Executive sponsor

This person cares about compounding advantage.

What they care about:

- AI adoption that improves the whole company, not just the most motivated individuals.
- Repeatable operating leverage.
- Speed, memory, institutional learning, and risk control.

Message:

> Make the best AI-enabled work reusable across the organization.

### Developer / open-source contributor

This person cares about hackability, architecture, integrations, and control.

What they care about:

- Open-source codebase.
- Self-hosted deployment.
- Provider-agnostic AI.
- MCP/connectors/integration surface.
- Extensible skills, routines, memory, and workflow primitives.

Message:

> A hackable, self-hosted AI workspace for teams that want to own the workflow layer.

## 5. Competitive Alternatives

The most important alternatives are not just direct product competitors. They are the default behaviors teams fall back into.

| Alternative | Why teams choose it | Where it breaks | Hearth frame |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Everyone uses AI tools independently | Fast, easy, already happening | Work does not compound; no shared workflows; context disappears | Turn individual AI leverage into team capability |
| Shadow AI | Teams can move quickly without waiting for approval | Risk, sprawl, duplicated work, no auditability | Govern AI adoption without killing momentum |
| AI lockdown | IT/security reduces risk by blocking tools | Teams lose productivity and route around policy | A third path between shadow AI and lockdown |
| ChatGPT/Claude team accounts | Familiar AI chat with admin controls | Useful work often remains chat-bound and vendor-bound | Make workflows, memory, decisions, and follow-up reusable |
| Prompt docs and internal wikis | Simple way to share tips | Static, stale, disconnected from actual work | Capture live workflows and activity, not just instructions |
| Project/task tools | Existing operating layer for work | They do not capture AI reasoning, context, or reusable workflows | Connect AI work directly to tasks, context, and routines |
| Workflow automation tools | Good for deterministic automations | Weak for collaborative AI reasoning, memory, and decision context | Combine AI collaboration, memory, and automation |
| Internal scripts and DIY agents | Highly customizable | Hard to share, govern, maintain, and adopt widely | Open-source platform with team-facing workflows |

## 6. Positioning Logic

### Competitive alternatives

If Hearth did not exist, teams would keep using a mix of individual AI accounts, Slack, docs, meetings, project management tools, workflow automation tools, internal scripts, and restrictive AI policies.

### Unique attributes

- Shared AI workspace for team collaboration.
- AI workflows that can become reusable team assets.
- Tasks and routines connected to AI conversations, docs, emails, Slack/Teams, meetings, and activity.
- Activity feed that helps teammates discover and adopt useful workflows.
- Company decision memory and context graph.
- Organization-owned AI memory instead of vendor-owned context lock-in.
- Self-hosting, provider choice, and bring-your-own LLM keys.
- Governance, compliance, auditability, and control.
- Open-source, extensible architecture with connectors and MCP.

### Value themes

- Individual AI wins become team-wide capability.
- Scattered work becomes reusable workflows.
- Lost context becomes owned organizational memory.
- AI sprawl becomes governed adoption.
- Manual follow-up becomes automated tasks and routines.
- Blank chats become proven starting points.

### Best-fit customers

Teams that already feel the productivity upside of AI but are starting to feel the organizational mess: repeated work, lost context, tool sprawl, governance concerns, and vendor lock-in risk.

### Market category

Primary external category:

> Open-source AI workspace for teams.

Supporting category language:

- AI workflow and memory platform.
- Self-hosted AI workspace.
- Team AI workspace.
- Governed AI adoption platform.

Avoid leading with:

- AI operating system.
- Compliance platform.
- Digital twin platform.
- Prompt management tool.

- Skills/routines.
- Activity feed.
- Task and artifact history.

### 2. Teamwork

Core message:

> AI work should be collaborative, not passed around as screenshots, prompt docs, and meeting notes.

What it means:

Hearth gives teams a shared workspace for AI-assisted work, so people can collaborate around the same context, tasks, decisions, artifacts, and routines.

Proof points:

- Real-time co-working on chats.
- Shared sessions and artifacts.
- Workspace/task board.
- Activity feed.

### 3. Memory

Core message:

> Stop losing decisions, context, and reasoning to scattered tools.

What it means:

Hearth captures the context your team creates while working with AI: decisions, artifacts, tasks, workflows, activity, and reasoning. The team can return to what happened, why it happened, and what should happen next.

Proof points:

- Memory system.
- Decision graph.
- Activity feed.
- Artifacts.
- Task context.

### 4. Ownership

Core message:

> Your company's AI memory should belong to your company.

What it means:

As teams use AI more, valuable context accumulates: prompts, decisions, files, workflows, feedback, preferences, and reasoning patterns. Hearth keeps that memory in infrastructure the organization controls, with provider choice instead of vendor dependence.

Proof points:

- Self-hosted deployment.
- Bring-your-own LLM keys.
- Provider-agnostic architecture.
- Organization-owned database and memory layer.
- Open-source codebase.

### 5. Automation

Core message:

> Turn AI conversations and team activity into follow-through.

What it means:

Hearth should help move work from discussion to execution: tasks, routines, schedules, workflow chains, approvals, and recurring team operations.

Proof points:

- AI-generated tasks.
- Routines.
- Event triggers.
- Schedules.
- Approvals.
- Connectors for Slack, email, meetings, and work systems.

### 6. Governance

Core message:

> The third path between shadow AI and AI lockdown.

What it means:

Teams need to use AI, but organizations need control. Hearth gives IT, CTOs, and InfoSec a governed path to adoption without forcing the business into unmanaged tools or blocking AI entirely.

Proof points:

- Self-hosting.
- Compliance packs.
- Governance policies.
- Audit/activity logs.
- Admin controls.
- Provider choice.

## 11. Claims And Proof

### Claims we can use now

- Open-source AI workspace for teams.
- Self-hosted.
- Bring your own LLM keys.
- Capture workflows, context, decisions, tasks, routines, and activity.
- Govern usage with admin/compliance controls.

### Claims that need product proof before heavy use

- "Under 5 minutes to value."
- "Connect Slack/Gmail/Teams/meetings and automatically create tasks."
- "Everyone can reuse the workflow from activity feed."
- "Digital teammate perspective" or "people perspective" claims.
- Enterprise-grade compliance language.

### Under 5 minutes to value standard

Do not use this claim prominently until the path is reliable from a clean install.

Required proof:

- One-command or near-one-command deployment works.
- LLM provider setup is clear and validated.
- Database migrations run automatically or are impossible to miss.
- A valid encryption key is generated or clearly provided.
- Setup wizard is routed into the first-run flow.
- Sample data or sample workflow creates an immediate "aha."
- First successful workflow can be completed by a new user in under 5 minutes after prerequisites.

Notes:

- "Digital twin" language may be useful later, but it should not lead. If used, frame it as an opt-in, evidence-grounded teammate perspective with governance and citations.
- "Private AI chats" should be avoided because it can sound scandalous, surveillance-coded, or related to privacy masking.
- "AI experiments" should be avoided because it can sound like research/lab work rather than real knowledge work.
