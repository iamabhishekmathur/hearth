## 0. The Skimmable Version

### Wedge positioning note

This bible now separates **the launch wedge** from **the full product surface**.

The launch wedge is the simplest hook:

> Hearth finds work assigned to you, gathers the context, plans the next steps, and has agents move it forward for your approval.

This is the story we lead with on the homepage and in first-touch GTM because it answers the reader's first question: **"How would I use this?"**

The full product is broader. Hearth still includes shared AI chat, tasks, routines, skills, activity/discovery, institutional memory, governance, cloud hosting, open-source self-hosting, and bring-your-own-model paths. Those surfaces matter, but they should support the wedge instead of competing with it above the fold.

Rule:

- **Lead with the wedge:** find, plan, finish work.
- **Expand into the platform:** shared chat, routines, skills, discovery, memory, governance, cloud, and self-hosting.
- **Do not cram the full product into the first sentence.**

### What Hearth is

Hearth is an open-source AI workspace that helps teams find, plan, and finish work.

It is not Slack. Slack is where teams talk. Hearth is where AI helps teams turn meetings, messages, docs, and team activity into owner-assigned work that can move forward with human approval.

It is also not just "shared ChatGPT." Shared AI chat is one surface. The bigger value is proactive work completion:

1. Hearth notices work assigned across meetings, Slack, Gmail, docs, and team activity.
2. Hearth gathers the context and turns the work into owner-assigned tasks.
3. AI breaks tasks into subtasks and performs the allowed work.
4. Humans provide input, approve, reject, or replan the final output.
5. Repeated work can become routines.
6. Useful workflows can be discovered, reused, and saved into company-owned memory.

### The plain-English pitch

Most companies have given people access to AI. That does not mean everyone is getting value from AI.

A few people figure out powerful workflows. They know what to ask, what tools to connect, what context to include, and how to turn AI output into actual work. Everyone else starts from a blank chat, falls behind, or waits for someone to send a Loom.

Hearth helps the whole team move from "I should ask AI" to "AI is already helping move the work forward."

It identifies assigned work, gathers context, plans subtasks, executes allowed work under human approval, and turns repeated work into routines teammates can reuse.

For end users, Hearth means:

- AI catches follow-up before it slips.
- AI finds work assigned to you across the places work already happens.
- AI gathers the context instead of making you paste it into a blank chat.
- AI plans the path and breaks work into subtasks.
- AI executes the allowed parts and brings the result back for approval.
- Recurring work can become automated routines.
- You can discover your teammates' workflows and add them to your own workflow.
- You no longer fall behind the AI power users on your team.

For executives and technical leaders, Hearth means:

- AI usage improves across the whole team, not just the power users.
- Company context, decisions, and history stay with the company.
- AI activity becomes visible, auditable, and governable.
- Teams get the third path between shadow AI and AI lockdown.
- Hearth Cloud gives speed and convenience.
- Open-source self-hosted Hearth gives maximum control.

### The simplest way to explain the product

Hearth has six core surfaces:

- **Chat:** shared AI sessions where teammates work with AI together.
- **Tasks:** owner-assigned work that AI breaks into subtasks and helps complete.
- **Routines:** AI-generated or user-created workflows that run on a schedule, trigger, or command.
- **Skills:** reusable capabilities or recipes that define how AI should do a specific kind of work.
- **Discovery Feed:** where teammates discover workflows others created and add them to their own workflow.
- **Institutional Memory:** company-owned context, decisions, artifacts, tasks, workflow history, and audit history.

## 3. Feature Definitions

### Chat

Chat is where teammates can work with AI together.

It is a useful surface, but it should not be the whole story. The product should be positioned around AI-assisted work, tasks, routines, discovery, and memory.

Plain description:

> Shared AI sessions for team work.

### Tasks

Tasks are owner-assigned.

AI can identify the work, break it into subtasks, perform parts of the work, and produce a final output. But a human owner remains responsible for approval.

Plain description:

> AI helps plan and execute the work. The human remains Approver-in-Chief.

What this means:

- Tasks have a human owner.
- AI proposes subtasks.
- AI executes where allowed.
- Humans can review, replan, approve, reject, or mark complete.
- Source context stays attached.
- Audit history matters.

### Routines

A routine is a workflow that gets generated, configured, and run.

It can run on a schedule, trigger, or command. It does not need to be tied one-to-one to a skill. A routine can use skills, tools, connectors, prompts, context, approval gates, and delivery steps.

Plain description:

> A workflow that Hearth can run for you repeatedly.

Examples:

- Every Monday, summarize launch risks.
- After each sales call, prepare follow-up and update the deal.
- When new support tickets arrive, cluster themes and create tasks.
- At the end of each week, summarize decisions and unresolved questions.

### Skills

Skill is the official product term.

Describe a skill like a recipe: a reusable way for AI to perform a specific capability or type of work.

Plain description:

> A reusable recipe for how AI should do something.

Examples:

- Turn call notes into product feedback.
- Generate a competitive brief.
- Review a contract for common risk areas.
- Convert customer objections into a sales follow-up.

### Discovery Feed

The Discovery Feed is where teammates discover each other's workflows.

It shows useful routines, skills, prompts, artifacts, and task workflows being created or used across the team. A teammate can inspect what worked and add it to their own workflow.

Plain description:

> Discover your teammates' workflows and add them to your own.

Preferred CTA:

> Add to my workflow.

### Institutional Memory

Institutional memory is the company-owned record of AI-assisted work.

It includes context, decisions, reasoning, artifacts, prompts, task history, workflow history, and audit history.

Plain description:

> The company's AI context, decisions, and history, owned by the company instead of the AI vendor.

Exec framing:

- The memory belongs to the organization.
- The company can choose model providers.
- The company can use Hearth Cloud for speed and convenience.
- The company can self-host for maximum control.
- The company can preserve decisions and context beyond individual employee accounts.

A team uses Hearth when work touches AI and should not depend on someone remembering to ask the perfect prompt.

Examples:

- Hearth sees a recurring pattern and suggests a routine.
- Hearth runs a sales follow-up workflow after a call.
- Hearth turns customer feedback into a product artifact and tasks.
- Hearth shows a teammate's routine in the Discovery Feed.
- Another teammate clicks "Add to my workflow."
- A CTO sees the institutional memory and audit trail.

### The "how will this transform me?" answer

For an AI power user:

> The workflows you already built become reusable workflows your team can add to their own work.

For a teammate who is not an AI power user:

> You no longer start from a blank chat or fall behind. Hearth finds useful work and lets you use what your teammates already figured out.

For a team lead:

> Follow-up becomes owner-assigned tasks, recurring work becomes routines, and useful workflows become shared.

For a CTO / CISO / IT leader:

> AI adoption becomes visible, governable, auditable, and owned by the organization.

### The first wow moment

The first wow should be proactive, not reactive.

The user should not have to ask: "Can you help me with this?"

Hearth should show:

1. It noticed work from Slack, Gmail, a meeting, a document, or a tool event.
2. It proposed an owner-assigned task or routine.
3. It broke the task into subtasks.
4. It used available tools/context to do the allowed work.
5. It returned the output for approval.
6. It offered to save or share the workflow.
7. A teammate discovered it and added it to their workflow.

That is the magic: Hearth finds work, moves it forward, and spreads what works.

### The "why now?" answer

AI access is no longer the bottleneck.

The bottleneck is whether teams can turn AI into proactive work completion:

- Can AI notice the work?
- Can AI create the task?
- Can AI run the routine?
- Can humans approve the output?
- Can teammates reuse what worked?
- Can leaders see productivity improve across the team?
- Can the company own the context and audit trail?

Hearth exists because the next phase of AI adoption is proactive, shared, governed, and team-wide.

## 6. Best-Fit Customers

### Launch ICP

AI-forward startups and SMB teams where a few people are already getting real value from AI, but the rest of the team has no systematic way to catch up.

The best early customer is not "a company curious about AI." It is a team that already has AI usage and is starting to feel the gap between power users and everyone else.

### Strong early segments

- Technical founders and startup teams.
- Product, engineering, operations, sales, success, support, recruiting, finance, and marketing teams with repeatable knowledge work.
- AI champions and internal power users.
- CTOs, CISOs, and technical operators who care about context ownership, auditability, self-hosting, provider choice, and governance.
- SMBs that want Hearth Cloud for speed and convenience.
- Teams that want open-source self-hosting for maximum control.

### Strong buying signals

- People share prompts, Claude projects, ChatGPT screenshots, AI tips, or Looms in Slack.
- A few people are clearly using AI more effectively than everyone else.
- The team has recurring work that could become routines.
- Follow-up is scattered across meetings, Slack, Gmail, docs, and task tools.
- Leaders worry AI productivity is uneven across the team.
- IT/security worries about shadow AI, vendor lock-in, sensitive context, PII, auditability, or AI tool sprawl.
- The team wants Cloud convenience now and self-hosting/control as a serious option.

### Weak initial fit

- Teams not using AI at all yet.
- Teams looking only for a single-purpose chatbot.
- Teams that want only project management.
- Teams that want only deterministic automation.
- Teams that want only compliance tooling, not a collaborative AI workspace.
- Teams that need verified enterprise compliance before any pilot and cannot evaluate a qualified early product.

## 14. Visual Direction

### The Handoff Visualization

This should become a core website and video visual.

Visual story:

```text
Team activity
  -> detected follow-up
  -> owner-assigned task
  -> AI-generated subtasks
  -> AI work output
  -> human approval
  -> routine / discovery / institutional memory
```

Purpose:

Show that Hearth is not "AI does everything unsupervised." Hearth is proactive AI-assisted completion with human ownership and approval.

### The Discovery Visualization

Visual story:

```text
Teammate creates workflow
  -> appears in Discovery Feed
  -> another teammate inspects it
  -> Add to my workflow
  -> run on their own work
```

Purpose:

Show that Hearth helps teammates learn from each other and avoid falling behind.

### The Institutional Memory Visualization

Visual story:

```text
Chats + tasks + routines + artifacts + decisions
  -> company-owned institutional memory
  -> audit and governance
  -> Hearth Cloud or self-hosted control
  -> swappable model providers
```

Purpose:

Show executives that the company owns the context layer, not the AI vendor.
